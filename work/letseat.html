UX/UI · Self-initiated Mobile App · 2021 Conceptual

One QR code.
Every restaurant's menu.

LetsEat is a self-initiated dine-in app concept I designed during the pandemic — a consistent, frictionless way for diners to browse any restaurant's menu from their own phone. No app download. No clunky mobile website. Just scan and browse.

01 — Context

Delivery apps unified the ordering experience. Dine-in didn't have an equivalent.

Uber Eats and SkipTheDishes solved a real problem: they gave delivery customers a consistent, well-designed experience regardless of which restaurant they were ordering from. The dine-in equivalent didn't exist.

The pandemic accelerated a behaviour that was already emerging — restaurants printing QR codes and directing diners to their mobile websites. But each restaurant's site was different. Some were hard to navigate. Most weren't designed for mobile. None of them felt like a product.

LetsEat is the answer to that gap: a single app that any restaurant can plug their menu into, giving every diner a consistent, polished browsing experience — regardless of where they're eating.

"Why does ordering delivery feel better than dining in? The food is supposed to be the good part."

This was a self-initiated project — brainstormed and designed solo during the pandemic, without a client brief or product team. The constraints that framing imposed (limited research access, no real restaurant partners) are acknowledged honestly in the scope section below.

02 — Problem

Three problems — all pointing to the same gap.

The move away from physical menus wasn't just a hygiene response. It revealed a deeper design problem that had existed long before the pandemic: the dine-in experience had never been given the same design attention as delivery.

Hygiene

Physical menus pass through dozens of hands between cleaning. Even before COVID, this was a real concern. The pandemic made it untenable.

Inconsistency

Every restaurant's mobile website is different. Different navigation, different layouts, different levels of quality. There's no shared experience to learn once and apply everywhere.

Experience gap

Delivery apps — Uber Eats, SkipTheDishes — have set a high bar for what browsing a menu feels like. Dine-in had nothing comparable. The same food, a worse experience.

03 — Process

Research first, then prototype fast.

Without access to restaurant owners or a large testing pool, the process leaned heavily on two sources: competitive analysis of existing food ordering apps, and paper prototype testing with a small group of regular diners.

The constraints were real — no restaurant partners, limited user testing during lockdown — but they shaped a leaner, more focused process. Every decision had to be justifiable from either the competitive research or the testing feedback.

1

Competitive analysis — delivery app UX patterns

Analysed Uber Eats, SkipTheDishes, DoorDash, and direct restaurant apps to identify the interaction patterns users had already internalised. The goal wasn't to copy delivery — it was to understand the mental model users would bring to a dine-in equivalent and design with that context, not against it.

2

User flowchart — mapping the dine-in journey

Mapped the end-to-end user flow from entering the app through to notifying the waiter. This step was important for surfacing moments where the digital experience intersected with physical dining — the QR scan, the waiter call — and designing those transitions intentionally rather than assuming they'd work.

3

Paper prototype + user testing

Printed wireframes were tested with a small group of people who regularly dine out. Testing at low fidelity kept the focus on flow and interaction patterns rather than visual polish. Two significant insights emerged that changed the design direction — both documented in the insights section below.

4

Moodboard + design direction

Established a black, white, and grey neutral palette — deliberately minimal so that the restaurant's own photography and food imagery could take the visual lead. The UI is the frame, not the subject.

5

High-fidelity prototype

Translated the tested wireframes and design direction into a full hi-fi prototype covering the core five-screen user flow — from onboarding through to order summary and waiter notification.

04 — Testing insights

Two findings from testing that changed the design.

The paper prototype testing surfaced two clear, actionable insights — both rooted in existing mental models users had built from delivery apps. Both changed the design.

This is the value of low-fidelity testing: the findings are about interaction, not aesthetics. Users could tell you what felt wrong before the visual design had any chance to distract them.

01

Users expected a bottom drawer — not a top-right basket — for reviewing their order.

Why it matters: Users had built a strong mental model from delivery apps where a persistent bottom sheet shows order details. A top-right basket icon required them to unlearn that pattern. The bottom drawer matches the existing expectation — less friction, faster comprehension.

Design response: Replaced the basket with a persistent bottom drawer that appears when the first item is selected, can be expanded for full order details, and collapsed to stay out of the way while browsing.

02

Users wanted to see how many of each item they'd selected — directly on the menu item, not just as a basket total.

Why it matters: A number badge on a basket icon tells you how many items total. It doesn't tell you how many of this specific dish you've added. When browsing a long menu, that distinction matters — users were second-guessing themselves unnecessarily.

Design response: Added a quantity badge directly on each menu item when it's been selected. The basket count still exists, but the per-item indicator reduces cognitive switching between the menu and the order summary.

05 — Design decisions

Three decisions that shaped the core experience.

Each decision was driven by either the competitive research, the testing insights, or a conscious constraint about what the product needed to be for restaurants and diners alike.

01

Bottom drawer over top basket

✦ Persistent bottom drawer

The bottom drawer pattern is established in delivery apps — users already know how to interact with it. It provides more real estate for order details when expanded, collapses cleanly when not needed, and keeps users oriented in the menu without navigating away. It matches the way people actually think about building an order: incrementally, with the ability to check back without losing their place.

Rejected: Top-right basket icon with badge counter. Testing showed users instinctively looked for the bottom drawer and were momentarily confused by the basket placement — even though it's a familiar pattern from e-commerce. The delivery app mental model was stronger.

02

Neutral B&W palette

✦ Black, white, and grey UI

LetsEat is infrastructure for restaurants — it needs to work for a French bistro, a ramen bar, a pizza chain, and a fine dining venue without feeling out of place in any of them. A strong branded color would compete with the restaurant's own identity. A neutral palette becomes an invisible frame, letting the restaurant's photography and food take center stage. The product disappears into the background — which is exactly what a platform should do.

Rejected: A branded accent color. Tested several warm and cool accent options during the moodboard phase — all of them created tension with certain restaurant photography styles. Neutral was the only option that worked universally.

03

Browser-based — no app install

✦ Progressive web app via QR scan

Requiring a diner to download an app before they can see a menu is too high a barrier. The whole premise of LetsEat is frictionless access — scan, browse, order. A native app install breaks that. A browser-based experience means the QR code is the only thing a restaurant needs to provide, and the first-time experience is instant. Repeat diners would benefit from a native install, but it should never be required.

Not considered viable: Native app install as a prerequisite. The ask — "download an app to see this menu" — would drive diners back to asking for a physical menu. Adoption depends on zero friction for first-time users.

06 — Key screens

Five screens. One end-to-end flow.

The core user journey — from QR scan to waiter notification — is covered in five screens. Each screen has a single primary job, and the transitions between them are intentional.

The hi-fi prototype was designed to give stakeholders and restaurant partners a tangible sense of the experience — and to pressure-test the interaction decisions from testing before any development investment.

07 — Scope & constraints

What this project is — and what it isn't.

LetsEat is a conceptual project, not a shipped product. It was self-initiated during the pandemic, without restaurant partners, a client, or a development team. That context is worth naming clearly — and the constraints it imposed were real.

Restaurant owner research was not possible during the project. Owners were managing closures, pivots to takeout, and constant regulation changes. They weren't available for interviews, and their priorities were nowhere near digital menu design.

User testing was limited to a small group of friends and family — people who dine out regularly but represent a narrow slice of the actual user population. The insights are valid, but the sample size makes them directional rather than conclusive.

Why I'm including this project

Conceptual projects reveal how a designer thinks when there's no brief, no client, and no one telling them what to build. This project shows problem identification, competitive research, iterative prototyping, and the ability to translate testing feedback into concrete design decisions — all self-directed. The constraints are honest; the process is real.

08 — Future opportunities

Where LetsEat could go next.

The current concept handles the core menu browsing and order notification flow. There are three clear directions for expansion — each with interesting design challenges and tradeoffs worth exploring.

The most interesting tension in the roadmap is the direct ordering question. It's more efficient — but it changes the social dynamic of dining out in ways that deserve careful research before assuming it's the right move.

Direct ordering

Allowing diners to place orders directly through the app — without calling a waiter — is the obvious efficiency gain. But it removes a social touchpoint that's part of the dining experience. The right answer probably varies by restaurant type: quick-service vs. fine dining have very different needs. This deserves research before designing.

Takeout + pager mode

LetsEat could extend to in-person takeout: browse the menu, place your order, and receive a pager notification when it's ready — allowing you to walk around rather than wait at the counter. The same core app, a different context of use. The primary design challenge is the status communication between kitchen and customer.

Restaurant owner tools

The diner experience is only half the product. Restaurant owners need to be able to publish and update their menu without engineering help. A companion CMS — simple enough for a restaurant owner to use between services — is a prerequisite for real adoption and the most underexplored part of the current concept.

The question I'd most want to answer

How do restaurant owners actually want to manage their menu content? The diner-facing experience is designed. What's missing is the supply side — understanding the operational reality of keeping a digital menu accurate and current. That research would determine whether LetsEat is a viable product or a useful prototype of an idea.

09 — Reflection

What I'd do differently.

The competitive analysis focused almost entirely on delivery apps — because that was accessible research. I'd expand that to include table-ordering systems used by restaurants (Square, Toast, Lightspeed) to understand the operational constraints on the restaurant side, not just the consumer experience.

I'd also run a second round of testing after the hi-fi prototype was complete — specifically testing the bottom drawer interaction at higher fidelity to confirm it worked as well as it did on paper. Wireframe testing validates the concept; prototype testing validates the execution.

What this project taught me.

Self-initiated projects are a different kind of challenge. Without a client or product team, every decision — what to build, what to cut, when to stop — is yours. That's not just a design exercise; it's a product instinct exercise.

The strongest outcome from this project wasn't the prototype — it was the clarity about what the product needed to be. A platform that disappears into the background, lets the restaurant's food take center stage, and respects the social context of dining out. Those three things shaped every visual and interaction decision, and they came from thinking carefully about the problem rather than jumping to solutions.
